What is the Challenge Recipe?

Write your learning goal and puzzle trick

Before we dive into building a webpage, let's talk about what makes a good challenge: all good challenges involve a puzzle.

Puzzles give us a sense of play, imply a sense of control over an environment and focus our attention to solve a logical problem. When you figure out how to solve a puzzle, you've essentially learnt something. This is why we're making puzzles in our challenges.

A good puzzle needs you to learn something in order to solve it. So we will need two things to get started: a learning goal and a puzzle trick.

Learning goal What you want the learners to learn by solving the puzzle.
Puzzle Trick The logical problem learners need to figure out to solve the puzzle and learn something new. e.g. the algorithms to solve a rubix cube depending on the current configuration.

Using those definitions as a guide, write the learning goal and puzzle trick for your puzzle. If you're stuck, check out this video where Scott Kim, Puzzlemaster, talks about different examples of puzzles.

Below is an example learning goal and puzzle trick for a challenge on Transforming Shapes. These are the foundation for our example challenge which we'll be using throughout the recipe.

Learning goal Understand how translate and rotate affect how shapes are drawn on the canvas.
Puzzle Trick Together rotate() and translate() position and orientate the canvas to any part of the screen. You draw one blade of the windmill, set the origin to the middle of the screen and then use rotate() to iterate the canvas orientation every time a for loop runs

Now go and write your own learning goal and puzzle trick. Your learning goal and puzzle trick will change and develop as you discover more about your challenge but you need to write something to give your idea an anchor.

When you've written a learning goal and puzzle trick, continue to the next page for a guide on sketching your puzzle. It's time to think about your puzzle from a human centred design perspective!

Sketch your puzzle

Human centredness asserts firstly, that we must always put people before machines, however complex or elegant that machine might be, and, secondly, it marvels and delights at the ability and ingenuity of human beings.
- Mike Cooley 2008

Now that you have a learning goal and a puzzle trick, it's time to think about your puzzle from a human centred design perspective. Your target user is a Design Programming student. To figure out what your target user needs, think about what sort of puzzle you and your friends would've wanted to help you learn when you took design programming.

With our target user in mind, it's time to sketch the puzzle. You’re figuring out what your completed puzzle looks like. We'll work backwards to turn this into a creative coding challenge in later steps.

When visualising your puzzle, consider patterns in nature, artistic influences or code projects on YouTube or GitHub as inspiration. To check if you’re on the right track, think about it in the terms that Scott (the puzzle-master from the previous step) puts forward; is there a clear problem to solve and is it fun?

Work out your algorithm

We've just sketched out what our puzzle will look like. But before we can figure out how the learner will solve the puzzle, we need to figure how the code will work for ourselves!

It might help to break down how it's going to work in a diagram before you develop it. Before you can teach this, you have to have working code for your solved puzzle. After you've got it working, we can break it down into learnable steps.

Using your sketch as a guide, work out how the instructions you give the computer in p5.js will create your solved puzzle. This is to break down the code process and keep track of the steps you take to get to the solved puzzle.

You can do this is by writing a code process flow. Below is an example of a code process flow for our example challenge on transforming shapes.

This is a process flow for our windmill challenge

This first box says we need to 'make a blade' and we think the best method to use would be beginShape(). It's also mentioned we might need to put this inside a custom function for later.

The second box says we need to 'make 6 blades' and we think a for loop will be the easiest way to do that. It also says we need to iterate the blade code 6 times to make enough blades for our windmill.

The third box says we need to 'rotate 6 blades' and we think adding an 'r' argument to our custom function will make it easy to rotate each blade the right amount by iterating the 'r' value in a for loop and then using 'r' as the value in the rotate method.

The fourth box says we need to 'translate 6 blades' and we think adding 'x' and 'y' arguments to the custom function will, like with 'r', make it easy to move each blade on the screen the right amount. This time, instead of the rotate method, we'll put 'x' and 'y' in the translate() method.

The final box says we have an extra challenge 'blow on blades'. We think the p5.audioIn method would be the way to hook up the windmill to our mic. We've also said there needs to be a blownBreeze variable and a gentleBreeze variable to mimic how regular wind and someone blowing on a windmill would combine to make the windmill move. Granted they'd have to have a powerful set of lungs to move a windmill but you get the idea. Then, it says the blownBreeze variable would be the mic amplitude and the gentleBreeze variable would use a counter to remain steady.

Woah! that was a lot of reading! Good job for sticking with it 😉.

Design your puzzle pieces

So you’ve just written the code for your puzzle. Well done! Now you need to break it down into learnable steps. These are what the learner will see and use to solve the puzzle.

The way you break down these steps won't be the same as your code process flow. You wrote that for yourself; someone who already had the skills you're trying to teach. Now you're writing it for a less experienced student. You're writing these steps for someone who doesn't understand the code yet. They will learn by solving your puzzle.

To bring it back to the user's perspective, think what kind of puzzle pieces you’d want if you were doing the challenge as a Design Programming student. Always keep your target user in mind.

Don't worry if this is confusing. Human Centred Design is like that for everyone at the start!

Write your challenge instructions

Now you’ve designed your puzzle pieces, it's time to write the instructions your learners will see so they understand what they need to do to complete your challenge.

Remember, this isn't just giving a learner the puzzle and telling them to solve it. You need to make sure they understand what's expected of them in each section of the challenge. It should be clear to them what they need to do. They read the instructions, complete what's asked of them and then move on to the next step.

The tricky part should be figuring out how to do it. That's where they learn something new.

In short, your instructions should clearly explain what the learner needs to do without explaining how to do it!

Start by writing your instructions in a word document. See if you can mimic the writing style in the example challenge i.e. splitting the challenge into sections, using titles for sections and providing a clear action for each section with support visual references.

Once you've written your instructions out, read it back and ask yourself, "Does this clearly explain what needs to be done without explaining how to do it?" If the answer is yes, you've written your instuctions!

Write your design justification

Now that you’ve made your web page it's time to justify your design. Go back through each step and reflect on why you think the decisions you made were good design choices. Then compile these reflections into your documentation according to the rubric.

If you come across something that should be designed differently according to what is the best design choice, go ahead and change it! This is what we call feedback based iteration and it's a skill you'll treasure no matter where you go in the design world.

Congratulations on reaching the end of the recipe and creating a puzzle. We hope that helping us teach programming has helped you learn more about it and get new students to fall in love with code!
